Today’s consumers have access to a wide variety of tools to manage their finances.

### 1. Budgeting Apps

These apps help track income, expenses, and savings goals.

**Features:**
- Linking bank and credit accounts to automatically import transactions
- Categorizing and visualizing spending
- Setting and tracking budgets for different categories

*Examples:* Mint, YNAB (You Need A Budget), EveryDollar

### 2. Investment Tools

App-based investment platforms enable small-scale investing.

**Features:**
- Low minimums (sometimes $0 to start)
- Robo-advisors use algorithms to manage investments based on risk preferences
- Educational resources and analysis tools

*Examples:* Robinhood, Acorns, Betterment

### 3. Credit Monitoring Tools

Monitor credit scores and get alerts to changes in your credit report.

*Examples:* Credit Karma, Experian, myFICO

### 4. Payment and Money Transfer Apps

Allow instant, secure transfers between users and easy payments at stores.

*Examples:* Venmo, PayPal, Zelle, Square Cash

### 5. Saving Tools

Automated savings and "round-up" features allow you to save without thinking.

*Example:* Chime, Digit, Qapital

---

### Example: Using a Budgeting App

Let’s say you sign up for Mint. Here’s how you might use it:

1. Connect your bank and credit accounts.
2. Mint imports transactions and categorizes them (“Groceries”, “Utilities”, “Shopping”).
3. Set monthly budgets for each category.
4. Mint emails you if you’re approaching or over your budget.
5. You review spending trends over months to identify areas to cut back.

### Privacy and Security in Financial Tools

- Use strong, unique passwords
- Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA)
- Regularly review account activity
- Avoid public Wi-Fi when accessing financial institutions
- Understand data sharing policies before linking accounts
