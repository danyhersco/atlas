Fundamental rights are those basic human rights that are recognized and guaranteed by constitutions and international law. They are often considered essential for individual dignity and the functioning of a democratic society. Although the exact list varies between jurisdictions, most modern constitutions and human rights documents share core similarities.

### 1. Civil and Political Rights

*Also referred to as “First Generation Rights”*

**Examples:**
- **Right to life and personal liberty:** Protection from arbitrary deprivation of life or liberty.
- **Freedom of speech and expression:** The right to express opinions without government interference.
- **Freedom of assembly and association:** The right to gather and form groups.
- **Right to a fair trial and due process:** Legal protections against arbitrary justice.

### 2. Economic, Social, and Cultural Rights

*“Second Generation Rights”*

**Examples:**
- **Right to education**
- **Right to work and fair wages**
- **Right to social security**
- **Right to health care**

### 3. Collective or Solidarity Rights

*“Third Generation Rights”* (Often found in newer constitutions and international instruments.)

**Examples:**
- **Right to self-determination**
- **Right to a clean environment**
- **Right to development**
- **Rights of minorities and indigenous peoples**

### Key Documents

Some landmark legal sources include:
- The United States Bill of Rights (1791)
- The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR, 1948)
- The European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR, 1950)
- National constitutions (e.g., India’s Fundamental Rights, South Africa’s Bill of Rights)
