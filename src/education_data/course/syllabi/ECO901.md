# Lecture 1: Introduction to Macroeconomics

## What is Macroeconomics?

Macroeconomics is the branch of economics that studies the behavior and performance of an entire economy, rather than individual markets. It focuses on aggregate indicators and the interactions between different sectors of the economy. Key domains include national income, overall output, unemployment, inflation, and growth.

**Some key questions in macroeconomics:**
- What determines the overall level of production in an economy?
- Why do economies grow over time?
- What causes economic recessions and expansions?
- How do policies like government spending and interest rates affect the economy?

### Example
Consider a country with millions of businesses and households. Microeconomics might examine how a single firm sets the price for its product, while macroeconomics is concerned with how all firms and households together determine the nation’s unemployment rate, total output, and price level.

## Microeconomics vs. Macroeconomics

Though both are part of economics, their scope and focus differ:

| Aspect               | Microeconomics             | Macroeconomics                     |
|----------------------|---------------------------|------------------------------------|
| Focus                | Individual agents (firms, households) | The economy as a whole         |
| Topics               | Supply and demand in markets, consumer choice, production costs | GDP, inflation, unemployment, monetary and fiscal policy |
| Example Question     | What will happen to the price of coffee if the price of milk rises? | What leads to national economic growth?                |

### Analogy

Think of microeconomics as examining the trees, while macroeconomics is surveying the entire forest.

### 1. What is National Output and How is it Measured?

# Lecture 2: Measuring Economic Performance: GDP and Beyond

## Introduction

In this lecture, we will examine how economists and policymakers measure the performance of an economy. Understanding these measures is fundamental for analyzing economic growth, business cycles, and standards of living. The primary focus will be on Gross Domestic Product (GDP)—its definition, calculation, and uses—followed by discussions of its limitations and alternative indicators.

---

## Gross Domestic Product (GDP): Definition and Calculation

**Gross Domestic Product (GDP)** is the most widely used indicator for measuring the economic activity of a country. It represents the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders over a specific period (usually one year or one quarter).

### GDP Definition

- **GDP:** The market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period.

**Key elements of GDP:**
- **Market Value:** GDP uses market prices as weights to sum up all goods and services.
- **Final Goods and Services:** Only final goods are included, to avoid double counting. Intermediate goods are not counted separately.
- **Produced Within a Country:** GDP counts all output produced within a country's borders, regardless of the nationality of the producer.
- **Given Period:** It is always measured over a specific time frame.

### Methods of Calculating GDP

There are three main approaches to calculating GDP. In a well-functioning system, all approaches yield the same value.

#### 1. **Production (Output) Approach**

Adds up the market value of all final goods and services produced in the economy:

$$
GDP = \sum \text{Value of Final Output}
$$

#### 2. **Expenditure Approach**

Summarizes total spending on the nation's output:

$$
GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)
$$

Where:
- $C$ = Consumption expenditure by households
- $I$ = Investment expenditure by firms
- $G$ = Government purchases
- $X$ = Exports
- $M$ = Imports

#### 3. **Income Approach**

Calculates the total income earned from production, including:
- Wages
- Rent
- Interest
- Profits

This is typically expressed as:

$$
GDP = Wages + Rents + Interest + Profits + Indirect Taxes - Subsidies + Depreciation
$$

**Example:**  
Suppose a country produces only bread and computers.

- 1000 loaves of bread at $2 each = $2,000
- 50 computers at $500 each = $25,000

Total GDP (production approach) = $2,000 + $25,000 = $27,000

---

## Types of GDP: Nominal vs. Real

GDP figures can be misleading if they do not account for changes in price levels over time. To tackle this, we distinguish between **nominal** and **real** GDP.

### Nominal GDP

- Measured in current prices, i.e., unadjusted for inflation.
- Reflects changes in both quantity and prices.

### Real GDP

- Adjusted for inflation.
- Uses prices from a base year (constant prices).
- Reflects changes in actual production/quantities.

**Formula for Real GDP:**

If $NGDP$ = nominal GDP, $P$ = price index (CPI or GDP deflator), $P_b$ = price index in base year:

$$
\text{Real GDP} = \frac{NGDP}{P} \times 100
$$

**Example:**

Suppose the nominal GDP in 2023 is $1,100$, and the GDP deflator is $110$ (base year = 100):

$$
\text{Real GDP} = \frac{1,100}{110} \times 100 = 1,000
$$

This means that if there had been no inflation, the GDP value would be $1,000$.

### GDP Deflator

The GDP Deflator measures the overall change in prices (price level) used to convert nominal GDP to real GDP:

$$
\text{GDP Deflator} = \frac{Nominal\ GDP}{Real\ GDP} \times 100
$$

---

## GDP Limitations and Alternative Measures

### Limitations of GDP

While GDP is an important and widely used measure, it has several limitations when used to assess economic well-being:

1. **Excludes Non-Market Activities**  
   Household production, volunteer work, and informal economies are not measured.

2. **Ignores Distribution of Income**  
   GDP per capita does not reveal income inequality.

3. **No Account of Environmental Degradation**  
   Resource depletion and environmental harm are not subtracted from GDP.

4. **Overlooks Leisure and Quality of Life**  
   Hours worked, free time, safety, and other aspects affecting well-being are not captured.

5. **Excludes Non-Recorded or Illegal Activities**  
   Shadow economies or black markets are not included.

**Illustration:**  
If an oil spill raises cleanup spending, GDP rises, even though overall well-being may fall.

### Alternative and Complementary Measures

To address these weaknesses, economists have proposed alternative indicators:

#### 1. **Gross National Product (GNP)**

- Measures total income earned by a country's residents, regardless of production location.
- Includes income from abroad, excludes income generated by foreigners domestically.

#### 2. **Net National Product (NNP) and National Income (NI)**

- NNP = GNP minus depreciation (wear and tear on capital).
- NI = NNP minus indirect taxes plus subsidies.

#### 3. **GDP per Capita**

- GDP divided by population.
- Useful for comparing living standards between countries.

#### 4. **Human Development Index (HDI)**

- Composite index developed by the United Nations.
- Combines indicators of income (GNI per capita), education, and life expectancy.

#### 5. **Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI)**

- Adjusts GDP by incorporating factors like income distribution, value of household and volunteer work, and costs of pollution and crime.

#### 6. **Green GDP**

- Adjusts GDP for environmental degradation and resource depletion.

---

## Other National Income Accounts

National income accounting involves other indicators that provide a fuller picture of economic activity:

### 1. **Personal Income (PI)**

- Total income received by households (before taxes).

### 2. **Disposable Personal Income (DPI)**

- Personal income minus personal taxes; income available for spending or saving.

### 3. **Gross National Income (GNI)**

- Similar to GNP, but used in international statistics.

**Hierarchy Recap:**
- GDP → GNP → NNP → NI → PI → DPI

---

# Lecture 3: Inflation: Concepts and Measurement

## What is Inflation?

Inflation is one of the central topics in macroeconomics and refers to the general increase in the price level of goods and services in an economy over a period of time. It captures the rate at which the purchasing power of money declines: if inflation is positive, every dollar buys fewer goods and services than before.

- **Definition**: Inflation is the percentage rate of change in the general price level from one period to the next.
- **Key Idea**: Inflation does **not** refer to an increase in a specific price, but rather to the **overall** price level.

**Example**:  
If in 2023, a basket of goods costs $100 and in 2024 it costs $105, the inflation rate is:
$$
\text{Inflation Rate} = \frac{105 - 100}{100} \times 100\% = 5\%
$$

### Types of Inflation

1. **Creeping (mild) inflation**: Low, steady rise in price level (e.g., 1-3% per year).
2. **Galloping inflation**: Rapid, often double- or triple-digit annual increases.
3. **Hyperinflation**: Extremely high, typically more than 50% per month, leading to collapse of the currency.
4. **Deflation**: The opposite of inflation; a sustained decrease in the general price level.

## Measuring Inflation: CPI and PPI

Economists use statistical indices to measure inflation. The two most widely used are the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the Producer Price Index (PPI).

### Consumer Price Index (CPI)

The **CPI** measures the average change over time in the prices paid by urban consumers for a fixed basket of consumer goods and services.

#### Steps in Calculating CPI

1. **Select a Base Year and Basket of Goods**: Identify a representative basket of goods and services purchased by typical consumers.
2. **Find Prices**: Determine current prices for all items in the basket.
3. **Calculate Cost of Basket**: Multiply quantities by prices, sum over all goods.
4. **Construct CPI**:
   $$
   \text{CPI}_t = \frac{\text{Cost of Basket in Year } t}{\text{Cost of Basket in Base Year}} \times 100
   $$
5. **Calculate Inflation**:
   $$
   \text{Inflation Rate} = \frac{\text{CPI}_{t} - \text{CPI}_{t-1}}{\text{CPI}_{t-1}} \times 100\%
   $$

**Example**:  
Suppose the basket costs $200 in 2022 (base year), and $210 in 2023.
- $CPI_{2022} = 200/200 \times 100 = 100$
- $CPI_{2023} = 210/200 \times 100 = 105$
- Inflation Rate $= (105 - 100)/100 \times 100\% = 5\%$

#### Uses and Limitations of the CPI

- Used to *index* wages and social security payments, and as a measure of cost of living.
- **Limitations**:
  - Substitution bias: The fixed basket doesn't account for consumers switching to cheaper goods.
  - Introduction of new goods.
  - Quality changes.
  - May not reflect individual household experiences.

### Producer Price Index (PPI)

The **PPI** measures average changes in prices received by domestic producers for their output. It is an indicator of price trends at an earlier stage in the production process.

#### Uses

- Seen as an early warning indicator of future consumer price inflation.
- Useful for businesses in planning and contract adjustments.

**Example**:  
If producer prices for steel rise, this might eventually show up as higher prices for cars (consumer goods).

### Core Inflation

**Core Inflation** is an adjusted measure that removes volatile items such as food and energy prices from the CPI or PPI, providing a clearer view of the underlying trend.

## Consequences of Inflation

Inflation has broad effects on the economy. Its consequences can be both anticipated and unanticipated.

### Anticipated Inflation

- **Menu Costs**: Costs to businesses of frequently updating prices.
- **Shoe Leather Costs**: Increased cost of transactions as people keep less cash and make more frequent withdrawals to avoid holding depreciating money.
- **Bracket Creep**: Taxpayers pushed into higher tax brackets due to nominal income rises, even if real income is unchanged.

### Unanticipated Inflation

- **Redistribution of Wealth**: Transfers wealth between debtors and creditors. If inflation is higher than expected, debtors benefit as they repay loans with "cheaper" dollars.
- **Uncertainty and Planning**: Businesses and consumers face more risk and may reduce investment and spending.
- **Distortion of Price Signals**: Makes it difficult to distinguish real price changes from inflation.

**Example**:  
If you borrow $1000 at 5% interest expecting low inflation, but inflation jumps to 10%, you return the $1000 plus $50 in interest, but those dollars are worth much less.

### Hyperinflation and Deflation

#### Hyperinflation

- Extremely rapid and out-of-control price increases.
- Mostly caused by excessive growth of money supply (often by governments printing money to cover deficits).
- Leads to collapse in use of money as a store of value, and often to "barter" economies.

**Historical Example**:  
Zimbabwe (2000s): Prices doubling daily, 100 trillion Zimbabwean dollar notes printed.

#### Deflation

- General fall in prices.
- Can be dangerous: consumers may delay purchases, leading to lower demand, higher unemployment, and further price falls (deflationary spiral).
- Examples: The Great Depression in 1930s USA.

## Real vs. Nominal Values

Inflation affects the interpretation of economic data over time.

- **Nominal value**: The monetary figure measured in current dollars.
- **Real value**: Adjusted for inflation; shows value in terms of purchasing power of a base year.

**Adjustment Formula**:
$$
\text{Real Value (in base-year dollars)} = \frac{\text{Nominal Value}}{\text{CPI Index/100}}
$$

**Example**:  
If your salary in 2023 is \$52,000 and $CPI_{2023} = 130$, then your real salary in base-year dollars is:
$$
\text{Real Salary} = \frac{52,000}{130/100} = \$40,000
$$

## Python Exercise: Calculating CPI

Below is a Python function to compute the CPI given two years of price data and fixed quantities for a basket of goods. Complete the function so that it works for any lists of prices and quantities.

```python
def calculate_cpi(prices_base, prices_current, quantities):
    """
    prices_base: list of prices in base year
    prices_current: list of prices in current year
    quantities: list of quantities for each good
    Returns: CPI (current year, base year = 100)
    """
    # Calculate total cost in base year
    cost_base = sum(p * q for p, q in zip(prices_base, quantities))
    # Calculate total cost in current year
    cost_current = sum(p * q for p, q in zip(prices_current, quantities))
    # Compute CPI
    cpi = (cost_current / cost_base) * 100
    return cpi

# Lecture 4: Unemployment and the Labor Market

## Introduction

Unemployment is one of the most scrutinized indicators in macroeconomics, and for good reason — it reveals much about the health of an economy and the welfare of its workforce. In this lecture, we will explore the fundamental aspects of unemployment, how it is measured, its various types, and the distinction between short-run fluctuations and the natural, long-run rate of unemployment.

---

## Understanding Unemployment

Unemployment refers to the condition of people who are able and willing to work, are actively seeking work, but cannot find employment. The unemployment rate is a key measure that captures this aspect of the economy, but understanding what lies beneath this simple statistic requires a deeper look.

### Key Labor Market Statistics

- **Labor Force**: The sum of all employed and unemployed individuals (who are actively seeking work) in the economy.
- **Employed**: Individuals who have worked for pay (or profit) during the reference period, or who have jobs from which they are temporarily absent.
- **Unemployed**: Individuals without work who are available for work and have actively sought employment in the recent past (usually the previous four weeks).
- **Not in the Labor Force**: Individuals who are neither employed nor actively seeking work (e.g., retirees, students not seeking work, homemakers).

     |

```

---

## Measuring the Unemployment Rate

The unemployment rate is a widely used metric, but its calculation hides important nuances.

### Unemployment Rate Formula

Let $U$ = number of unemployed people, $L$ = number of people in the labor force.

$$
\text{Unemployment Rate} = \frac{U}{L} \times 100\%
$$

#### Example

If a country has 1,000 people in the labor force, and 90 are unemployed:

$$
\text{Unemployment Rate} = \frac{90}{1000} \times 100\% = 9\%
$$

#### Labor Force Participation Rate

The fraction of the adult population that is in the labor force.

Let $N$ = adult population.

$$
\text{Labor Force Participation Rate} = \frac{L}{N} \times 100\%
$$

#### Example

If the adult population is 1,200, then:

$$
\text{Labor Force Participation Rate} = \frac{1000}{1200} \times 100\% = 83.3\%
$$

### Limitations of the Unemployment Rate

- **Discouraged Workers:** Those who have stopped searching for jobs are not counted as unemployed.
- **Underemployment:** Part-time workers who want full-time jobs and people working below their skill levels are counted as employed.
- **Misreporting:** Survey respondents may misclassify their status.

---

## Types of Unemployment

Not all unemployment is the same. Economists classify unemployment into several categories, each with different causes and implications.

### 1. Frictional Unemployment

**Definition:** Short-term unemployment that arises from the normal process of matching workers with jobs.

- **Causes:** Workers transitioning between jobs, recent graduates entering the labor force, people relocating.
- **Characteristics:** Usually considered inevitable and even desirable as it reflects labor mobility.

**Example:** A software developer voluntarily leaves her job to find one that better matches her skills.

---

### 2. Structural Unemployment

**Definition:** Unemployment due to a mismatch between workers’ skills and the requirements of the available jobs.

- **Causes:** Technological change, shifts in consumer demand, trade competition, changes in labor regulations.
- **Characteristics:** Usually longer-lasting and more problematic than frictional unemployment.

**Example:** Factory workers lose jobs when machines automate the production process, and they lack the skills to operate or maintain the new technology.

---

### 3. Cyclical Unemployment

**Definition:** Unemployment that arises from downturns in the business cycle — i.e., from a lack of aggregate demand.

- **Causes:** Recessions and economic slowdowns.
- **Characteristics:** Tied closely to the health of the economy; can rise or fall quickly as the economy contracts or expands.

**Example:** Restaurant servers are laid off during a recession when people cut back on dining out.

---

### 4. Seasonal Unemployment

**Definition:** Unemployment caused by seasonal fluctuations in demand for certain kinds of labor.

- **Example:** Agricultural workers may be unemployed during the off-season; retail employees may find their hours reduced after the holiday season.

---

## Short-Run vs. Long-Run Unemployment

It is important to distinguish between short-run, cyclical movements in unemployment and its longer-term, “natural” rate.

### Short-Run Unemployment

- Subject to business cycles.
- Heavily influenced by changes in aggregate demand.
- Fluctuates above and below the natural rate.

**Illustration:** After a financial crisis, firms reduce hiring and lay off workers, leading to a sharp increase in cyclical unemployment.

### The Natural Rate of Unemployment

- The “normal” level of unemployment around which the actual rate fluctuates.
- Composed of frictional and structural unemployment.
- Exists even in a healthy, growing economy.
- Also known as the “full employment” rate of unemployment.

#### Why is Unemployment Never Zero?

Even in the best-performing economies, there is always some level of frictional and structural unemployment:

- People are always moving between jobs (frictional).
- Economy constantly changes, rendering some skills obsolete (structural).

### The Long-Run Perspective

In the long run, the natural rate of unemployment reflects labor market institutions, job search behavior, regulation, technology, and demographics. Policies aimed at reducing unemployment to zero are neither realistic nor desirable, as some turnover is welcomed and healthy.

---

## The Unemployment Insurance Effect

Unemployment insurance can help reduce the hardship caused by unemployment, but it may also increase the duration of job searches, raising the measured unemployment rate.

**Trade-off:** Provides income security at the expense of potentially slowing the match between workers and jobs.

---

## Policy Implications

- **Macro policies** (fiscal and monetary) can address cyclical unemployment.
- **Education, retraining, and labor mobility policies** can reduce structural unemployment.
- **Job information services and reduced regulation** can lower frictional unemployment.

# Lecture 5: The Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply Model

## Introduction

Welcome to Lecture 5 of ECO901: Macroeconomics—Growth and Fluctuations. In the past lectures, we've developed essential tools for measuring macroeconomic performance such as GDP, inflation, and unemployment. Today, we introduce one of the central frameworks for analyzing the economy as a whole: the Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply (AD-AS) model. 

The AD-AS model extends earlier concepts to explain:
- How national output (GDP), price levels (inflation), and unemployment are jointly determined.
- The causes of short-run macroeconomic fluctuations (the business cycle) and long-run growth.

---

## Aggregate Demand: Components and Determinants

### What is Aggregate Demand?

Aggregate Demand (AD) is the total quantity of goods and services demanded across all levels of an economy at a given overall price level and in a given time period.

Formally, it is the sum of expenditures on domestic goods and services:
$$
AD = C + I + G + NX
$$

Where:
- $C$ = Consumption
- $I$ = Investment
- $G$ = Government spending
- $NX$ = Net exports (Exports - Imports)

### The Aggregate Demand Curve

The AD curve represents the relationship between the total quantity of goods and services demanded (on the horizontal axis) and the price level (on the vertical axis).

**Why is the AD Curve downward sloping?**

1. **Wealth (Real Balances) Effect:** 
   - As the price level falls, the real purchasing power of money increases, encouraging higher consumption.

2. **Interest Rate Effect:** 
   - A lower price level reduces the demand for money, leading to lower interest rates, which encourages investment and some types of consumption.

3. **International Trade Effect:**
   - A lower domestic price level makes exported goods cheaper for foreigners, increasing exports (NX), and makes imports more expensive, reducing imports.

**Example:**
Suppose the overall price level in the economy drops. Consumers feel wealthier, interest rates fall so borrowing for investment is cheaper, and domestic goods become more competitive abroad. All these effects raise aggregate demand.

### Determinants of Aggregate Demand

- **Changes to $C$, $I$, $G$, or $NX$ shift the AD curve:**
    - Increase in consumer confidence → higher $C$ → AD shifts right.
    - Higher government spending ($G$) → AD shifts right.
    - A recession in trading partners reduces $NX$ → AD shifts left.

**Other Factors:**
- Fiscal policy: Changes in taxes and government spending.
- Monetary policy: Changes in money supply influence interest rates.
- Exchange rates: Affect competitiveness and net exports.

---

## Aggregate Supply: Short-Run vs. Long-Run

### What is Aggregate Supply?

Aggregate Supply (AS) is the total quantity of goods and services firms in an economy are willing to produce at various price levels.

**Distinction:**
- **Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS):** Some input prices (like wages) are sticky and do not adjust immediately.
- **Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS):** Input prices are fully flexible; output depends only on productive resources, technology, and institutions—**not** on the price level.

### The Short-Run Aggregate Supply Curve (SRAS)

- **Shape:** Upward sloping.
- **Reason:** When prices of final goods/services rise but input prices are sticky, firms' profits rise, so they increase production.

**Example:**
If the price level rises unexpectedly, but wages are based on past expectations and remain unchanged, firms can earn higher profits by producing more.

### The Long-Run Aggregate Supply Curve (LRAS)

- **Shape:** Vertical at potential (full-employment) output.
- **Reason:** In the long run, all prices and wages are flexible. Output is determined by resources and technology, not by the price level.

**Potential Output:**
- The economy’s sustainable level of output, also called natural output or full-employment output ($Y^*$).

---

## Macroeconomic Equilibrium

### Finding Equilibrium

Equilibrium occurs where AD intersects with AS. 

- **Short-Run Equilibrium:** Where AD meets SRAS. May be above or below potential output.
- **Long-Run Equilibrium:** Where AD, SRAS, and LRAS intersect at the same point—output is at its potential.

**Graphical Representation:**

![AD-AS Model](https://i.imgur.com/iwGMpIz.png)

- **$Y^*$:** Potential (full-employment) output
- **$P_e$:** Equilibrium price level

### Output Gaps

- **Recessionary (Negative) Gap:** If equilibrium output is below potential, unemployment is high.
- **Inflationary (Positive) Gap:** If equilibrium is above potential, output is unsustainably high, and inflation pressures build up.

---

## Shifts in AD/AS Curves and Economic Outcomes

### Shifts in Aggregate Demand

- **Expansionary AD Shift (Right):**
    - Causes: Lower taxes, more government spending, increased investment or exports.
    - Result (Short-Run): Higher output ($Y$), higher price level ($P$), possible positive output gap.

- **Contractionary AD Shift (Left):**
    - Causes: Higher taxes, less government spending, decreased private investment.
    - Result: Lower output and price level, possible recessionary gap.

### Shifts in Aggregate Supply

- **SRAS Shifts Right:** 
    - Causes: Lower input costs (like oil prices), improved productivity.
    - Result: Higher output, lower price level.

- **SRAS Shifts Left (Adverse Supply Shock):**
    - Causes: Natural disasters, higher wage costs, higher raw material prices.
    - Result: Lower output, higher price level (stagflation).

### Illustrating Shocks with Diagrams

Let's look at **expansionary fiscal policy**:
- Government boosts spending.
- AD shifts right.
- Output rises above potential.
- Price level increases.

Now consider a **negative supply shock**:
- Oil prices spike.
- SRAS shifts left.
- Output falls, price level rises (stagflation).

# Lecture 6: Business Cycles: Fluctuations in the Economy

## The Phases of the Business Cycle

A **business cycle** refers to the fluctuations in economic activity that an economy experiences over time. These cycles are characterized by periods of expansion and contraction in GDP, employment, and other macroeconomic variables.

### Four Main Phases

1. **Expansion (Recovery)**
   - Economic activity increases.
   - Output, income, employment, and consumer spending rise.
   - Usually accompanied by increasing investment and consumer confidence.

2. **Peak**
   - The end of the expansion phase.
   - Maximum output is reached.
   - The economy is typically at or near full employment; inflation may start to rise.

3. **Contraction (Recession)**
   - Economic activity slows down.
   - GDP, employment, and spending decrease.
   - Investments decline and consumer confidence drops.
   - If severe and prolonged, called a **depression**.

4. **Trough**
   - The lowest point of economic activity.
   - Marks the end of the recession.
   - Signals the beginning of the next expansion phase.

**Visualizing the Cycle**:

```
GDP
 ^
 |               /\
 |             /    \
 |           /        \
 |         /            \
 |--------/                \---------
       Expansion  Peak  Recession  Trough
(Time) ----------------------------------->
```

## Common Indicators of Economic Activity

Economists use several statistical indicators to track and predict the business cycle. These indicators can be classified as:

- **Leading Indicators**: Predict future movement (e.g., stock market returns, new orders for durable goods, building permits).
- **Coincident Indicators**: Move simultaneously with the overall economy (e.g., GDP, income, employment).
- **Lagging Indicators**: Change after the economy has already begun a new trend (e.g., unemployment rate, corporate profits).

### Example Indicators

- **Gross Domestic Product (GDP)** (Coincident): Measures the value of all goods and services produced.
- **Unemployment Rate** (Lagging): Rises after the economy has entered a recession; falls after expansion is underway.
- **Consumer Confidence Index** (Leading): Changes before the economy as a whole moves in a new direction.

## Theories of Business Cycles

To understand why business cycles occur, economists have proposed several theories:

### Classical Theory

- Assumes markets clear automatically; prices and wages adjust to restore equilibrium after shocks.
- Cycles are the result of temporary shocks (e.g., weather, technology).
- Little role for government intervention.

### Keynesian Theory

- Advocated by John Maynard Keynes.
- Argues that aggregate demand fluctuations cause business cycles.
- Prices and wages are "sticky" (slow to adjust).
- Supports the use of fiscal and monetary policy to stabilize the cycle.

### Monetarist Theory

- Associated with Milton Friedman.
- Argues that fluctuations in the money supply drive business cycles.
- Too much money causes booms; too little causes recessions.
- Advocates steady growth of money supply.

### Real Business Cycle (RBC) Theory

- Focuses on "real" shocks (technology, productivity, resource availability).
- Assumes flexible prices and rational expectations.
- Business cycles are efficient responses to real changes, not market imperfections.

### Austrian Theory

- Cycles arise from distortions in interest rates, usually because of inappropriate credit expansion by central banks.
- Emphasizes the role of malinvestments during booms that are corrected during busts.

## Causes of Economic Fluctuations

Economic fluctuations, or "shocks," can be:

### Demand-Side Shocks

- Changes in consumer or business confidence.
- Government spending or taxation changes.
- Monetary policy changes (interest rates, money supply).

**Example:** The 2008 financial crisis began with a drop in consumer and business confidence, reinforced by contractionary credit conditions.

### Supply-Side Shocks

- Changes in the availability or price of resources (oil shocks).
- Technological advances or failures.
- Natural disasters.

**Example:** The oil crises of the 1970s led to higher production costs and stagflation (high inflation and stagnation).

### External Shocks

- International events, like wars, global pandemics, or foreign recessions.
- Changes in global demand/supply for key goods.

## Consequences of Economic Fluctuations

Business cycles have real impacts on people and the economy:

- **Employment**: Recessions bring higher unemployment; expansions bring job growth.
- **Output**: National output falls in contractions, rises in expansions.
- **Investment**: Firms cut investment in downturns, invest more when optimistic.
- **Inflation**: Can rise in booms (demand-pull inflation) and fall or even turn into deflation during busts.
- **Government Finances**: Tax revenues fall and social spending rises during recessions, potentially increasing deficits.

### Social Consequences

- Higher unemployment and business failures.
- Increased anxiety and reduced well-being among the population.

## Case Study: The Great Recession (2007-2009)

- **Phase**: Started as an expansion, peaked in late 2007, and entered a severe contraction.
- **Causes**: Burst of the housing bubble, banking crisis, sharply falling consumer confidence.
- **Consequences**: Global output fell, unemployment soared, governments responded with stimulus packages.

## The Role of Policy

Governments and central banks often use **countercyclical policies** to smooth the business cycle:

- **Fiscal Policy**: Changes in government spending and taxes.
- **Monetary Policy**: Adjustment of interest rates and money supply.
- **Automatic Stabilizers**: Unemployment benefits and progressive taxes adjust automatically with the cycle.

**Key Insight:** Policies are more effective at mitigating the depths of contractions than eliminating cycles altogether.

# Lecture 7: Economic Growth: Sources and Measurement

## What is Economic Growth?

Economic growth refers to the increase in the value of goods and services produced by an economy over time. It is a central goal of most macroeconomic policies, as sustained growth leads to higher living standards, greater employment opportunities, and improved public services.

Growth is often discussed in two forms:
- **Short-run growth:** Year-to-year increases in output, often due to fuller use of existing resources (reducing unemployment, increasing capacity utilization).
- **Long-run growth:** An upward shift in the economy's productive capacity, often tied to increased resources, technological advances, or improvements in productivity.

**Key Concept:**
- Economic growth is conventionally measured as the percentage increase in Real Gross Domestic Product (GDP) or Real GDP per capita over time.

## Measuring Growth and Productivity

### Real GDP and Real GDP per Capita

- **Real GDP:** Measures the value of all goods and services produced, adjusted for changes in price level (inflation).
- **Real GDP per capita:** Divides real GDP by the population, providing insight into average living standards.

### Growth Rate Calculation

The growth rate of real GDP (or per capita real GDP) is calculated as:

$$
\text{Growth Rate} = \frac{\text{GDP}_{\text{current}} - \text{GDP}_{\text{previous}}}{\text{GDP}_{\text{previous}}} \times 100\%
$$

**Example:**
Suppose a country's real GDP was $500 billion in year 1 and $525 billion in year 2.
$$
\text{Growth Rate} = \frac{525 - 500}{500} \times 100\% = 5\%
$$

### Productivity

Productivity is a key determinant of economic growth and refers to the amount of output produced per unit of input, typically per worker or per hour worked.

- **Labor Productivity:** 
$$
\text{Labor Productivity} = \frac{\text{Total Output}}{\text{Number of Workers or Hours Worked}}
$$

Improvements in productivity allow more goods and services to be produced with the same amount of labor and resources, fueling higher growth without simply increasing resource use.

> **Key Difference:** Output can grow by employing more people (extensive growth) or by making each worker more productive (intensive growth). Sustainable long-run growth relies on increases in productivity.

## Determinants of Economic Growth

Economic growth is influenced by several broad factors:

### 1. Physical Capital Accumulation

**Physical capital** refers to the stock of equipment, machinery, buildings, and infrastructure available to produce goods and services. Increased investment in capital (e.g., building new factories, purchasing machines) increases the capacity for production.

- **Capital deepening:** Increasing the amount of capital per worker, often leading to higher productivity.

### 2. Human Capital

**Human capital** is the knowledge, skills, and health that workers bring to production. Investment in education, training, and healthcare leads to a more skilled and productive workforce.

**Examples:**
- Public education systems
- On-the-job training
- Better nutrition and healthcare

### 3. Technological Progress

Technological progress refers to improvements in how resources are combined to produce output, often through innovation or adoption of better methods and tools. This is seen as the engine of long-term growth.

- **Examples:**
  - Development of new machinery (e.g., computers, automation)
  - Improved organizational methods (e.g., assembly line production)
  - Advances in digital technology

### 4. Institutional Factors

Institutions—such as property rights, rule of law, political stability, and efficient markets—create incentives and conditions that support investment, innovation, and efficient allocation of resources.

**Examples:**
- Effective contract enforcement encourages investment.
- Stable monetary policy reduces uncertainty.

### 5. Natural Resources

While the presence of abundant natural resources (oil, minerals, fertile land) can support growth, they are not a guarantee of development. How resources are managed and utilized is crucial.

**Caution:** Some countries with many resources still experience low growth due to poor governance or lack of diversification (the "resource curse").

### 6. Openness to Trade

Economies that are open to international trade can benefit from larger markets, increased competition, technology transfer, and access to capital.

## The Role of Technology and Capital

### The Solow Growth Model (Basics)

The Solow growth model is a framework used to understand the mechanics of economic growth over time, emphasizing capital accumulation, labor or population growth, and technological advancement.

In its simplest form, output (Y) depends on capital (K), labor (L), and technology (A):

$$
Y = A \cdot F(K, L)
$$

Where:
- $Y$: Output (GDP)
- $K$: Physical capital
- $L$: Labor input
- $A$: Total factor productivity (technology)

**Key implications:**
- Increases in $K$ and $L$ raise output, but with diminishing returns.
- Sustained long-term growth in output per worker requires technological progress (increasing $A$).

### Capital Deepening vs. Technological Progress

- **Capital deepening:** More capital per worker boosts output, but due to diminishing returns, its effect lessens over time.
- **Technological progress:** Shifts the production function upward, allowing for continued growth in output per worker even without increases in capital, largely overcoming the limits imposed by diminishing returns.

### Example: Growth Accounting

Suppose a country increases the number of machines (K), its workforce (L), and also introduces new technology (A). Output rises, but growth accounting can decompose what proportion of the increase comes from:
- More machines
- More workers
- Smarter production (technology)

If labor force and capital stock have barely grown, but output grows quickly, technological progress must be an important source.

# Lecture 8: Government Policy: Fiscal Tools

## Fiscal Policy Defined

**Fiscal policy** refers to the use of government spending and taxation to influence a country's economic activity. It is one of the primary tools, alongside monetary policy, that governments use to steer the economy toward desired objectives such as full employment, price stability, and economic growth.

There are two main types of fiscal policy:
- **Expansionary fiscal policy**: Increases aggregate demand through higher government spending or lower taxes, typically employed during recessions.
- **Contractionary fiscal policy**: Decreases aggregate demand by reducing government spending or increasing taxes, usually adopted to combat inflation.

Fiscal policy decisions are typically made through the governmental budget process and are implemented via changes to spending programs and tax regulations.

## Government Spending and Taxation

Fiscal policy operates chiefly through two levers:

### Government Spending

This encompasses all government expenditures on goods and services, including:
- Infrastructure (roads, bridges, public transportation)
- Health care and education
- Public safety and national defense
- Social programs (unemployment benefits, social security)

In macroeconomic terms, government spending ($G$) is a component of aggregate demand:
$$
AD = C + I + G + (X - M)
$$
Where:
- $C$ = consumption
- $I$ = investment
- $G$ = government spending
- $X$ = exports
- $M$ = imports

**Example:** If the economy slides into a recession, the government might increase spending on infrastructure projects. This not only creates jobs directly but may also have multiplier effects throughout the economy as workers spend their wages.

### Taxation

Taxation determines how much money households and businesses have available to spend or invest. Taxes may include:
- Direct taxes (income tax, corporate tax)
- Indirect taxes (sales tax, value-added tax)

A reduction in taxes increases disposable income ($Y_d$), stimulating consumption ($C$):
$$
Y_d = Y - T
$$
Where:
- $Y$ = total income
- $T$ = taxes

**Example:** Cutting payroll taxes during an economic downturn leaves households with more take-home pay, encouraging higher spending.

### The Fiscal Multiplier

The impact of government spending or taxation on national income is measured by the **fiscal multiplier**.

If $G$ increases by $1$, and the multiplier is $k$, total output increases by $k$ dollars:
$$
\Delta Y = k \Delta G
$$
The size of the multiplier depends on the marginal propensity to consume (MPC), tax rates, and other factors.

## Deficits, Surpluses, and Public Debt

### Fiscal Deficits

A **budget deficit** occurs when government expenditures exceed tax revenues in a given period.

$$
\text{Deficit} = G - T \quad \text{(if } G > T \text{)}
$$

**Financing Deficits:**
- Borrowing by issuing government bonds
- Monetizing the deficit (less common; involves financing via central bank)

### Fiscal Surpluses

A **budget surplus** arises when tax revenues exceed government expenditures:

$$
\text{Surplus} = T - G \quad \text{(if } T > G \text{)}
$$

### Public Debt

The accumulated deficits (minus any surpluses) over time make up the **public or national debt**.

$$
\text{Debt}_{t} = \text{Debt}_{t-1} + \text{Deficit}_{t}
$$

Governments manage debt through further borrowing, refinancing, or by using surpluses to pay it down.

#### Debt-to-GDP Ratio

A common measure of a country's fiscal health is its **debt-to-GDP ratio**:

$$
\text{Debt-to-GDP Ratio} = \frac{\text{Total Public Debt}}{\text{GDP}}
$$

A high ratio may raise concerns about fiscal sustainability and affect a nation's borrowing costs.

**Example:** In 2020, many countries saw their debt-to-GDP ratios rise sharply due to increased spending during the COVID-19 pandemic and declining tax revenues.

## Fiscal Policy’s Impact on Growth and Stability

### Stabilizing the Business Cycle

Fiscal policy seeks to smooth the business cycle through countercyclical measures:
- **During Recessions:** Implement expansionary fiscal policy (increase spending, cut taxes).
- **During Booms:** Use contractionary fiscal policy (decrease spending, increase taxes).

### Short-Run and Long-Run Effects

- **Short-run:** Fiscal policy primarily influences aggregate demand.
- **Long-run:** Sustained deficits and rising public debt can “crowd out” private investment, potentially reducing economic growth.

### Crowding Out

If government borrowing raises interest rates, it may reduce private investment—a phenomenon called **crowding out**.

**Example:**
Let’s say the government needs to borrow more. This increases demand for loanable funds, pushing up the interest rate, making it more expensive for private businesses to borrow and invest.

### Fiscal Policy and Economic Growth

Well-targeted fiscal policy can stimulate long-term growth by:
- Investing in infrastructure
- Funding education and research
- Supporting innovation

Poorly executed fiscal policies, or excessive deficits, can hinder growth by:
- Raising interest rates
- Increasing future tax burdens
- Destabilizing public finances

### Automatic Stabilizers

Some fiscal mechanisms kick in automatically as economic conditions change, without new government action. These **automatic stabilizers** include:
- Progressive income taxes (tax revenues fall in recessions, rise in booms)
- Unemployment benefits (increase during downturns, decrease in expansions)

These help dampen the amplitude of business cycle fluctuations.

### Discretionary Fiscal Policy

**Discretionary policy** refers to deliberate changes in spending or taxation by the government (new laws or budgets), as opposed to automatic stabilizers.

#### Implementation Lags

Fiscal policy suffers from **lags**:
- *Recognition Lag*: Time to identify economic changes.
- *Decision Lag*: Time to pass legislation.
- *Implementation Lag*: Time to put changes into action.

For this reason, timing and targeting are essential for effective fiscal interventions.

## Example: Fiscal Policy in the Global Financial Crisis

During the 2008-2009 crisis, many countries enacted large fiscal stimulus packages to counteract declining private demand. This included:
- Major infrastructure projects
- Tax cuts and direct payments to households
- Increased social spending

Studies showed that these policies helped stem deeper recessions, but also contributed to rising public debts.

# Lecture 9: Government Policy — Monetary Tools

## Introduction to Monetary Policy

Monetary policy is one of the two primary types of macroeconomic policy used by governments to influence the economy, the other being fiscal policy (covered in the previous lecture). While fiscal policy involves changes in government spending and taxation, **monetary policy** refers to the management of a nation's money supply and interest rates—primarily by a country's central bank—to achieve macroeconomic objectives such as controlling inflation, managing employment levels, and fostering economic growth.

Monetary policy plays a crucial role in maintaining economic stability and steering economic growth. Let's delve deeper into how this important lever of government works.

## The Role of Central Banks

At the center of monetary policy is the **central bank**—for example, the Federal Reserve (Fed) in the United States, the European Central Bank (ECB) in the Eurozone, or the Bank of England in the UK.

**Central banks** are responsible for:

- Issuing and regulating the national currency
- Controlling the supply of money in the economy
- Setting key interest rates that influence all other rates in the economy
- Acting as a lender of last resort to the banking system

Central banks are generally independent from direct government control, to avoid political influence over crucial economic decisions.

### Objectives of Central Banks

Most central banks have one or more of these mandates:

- **Price Stability**: Keeping inflation low and steady.
- **Maximum Employment**: Promoting high employment levels.
- **Economic Growth and Stability**: Smoothing out business cycles and avoiding deep recessions.

For example, the U.S. Federal Reserve's dual mandate is to achieve "maximum employment" and "stable prices."

## Money Supply and Its Measurement

Central banks influence the **money supply**—the total amount of money available within an economy.

### Components of the Money Supply

Broadly, economists classify money into different categories:

- **M1**: Currency in circulation + demand deposits (checking accounts).
- **M2**: All of M1 + savings accounts, small time deposits, and other near monies.

Central banks keep close tabs on these measures to inform policy decisions.

### Tools to Control the Money Supply

Central banks use several **tools of monetary policy**:

1. **Open Market Operations (OMOs)**: Buying or selling government securities (like bonds) in the open market to control the amount of money circulating.
2. **Discount Rate**: The interest rate central banks charge commercial banks for short-term loans.
3. **Reserve Requirements**: The fraction of deposits that commercial banks must hold in reserve, rather than lend out.

Let’s see how each tool works.

#### Open Market Operations (OMOs)

**Most frequently used tool.**
- **Buying government bonds** increases the money supply (injects money into the economy).
- **Selling government bonds** decreases the money supply (withdraws money from the economy).

##### Example

Suppose the Fed wants to stimulate a sluggish economy. It buys $50 billion in government bonds from banks. This gives banks more cash to lend, leading to more loans and increased economic activity.

#### Discount Rate

- If the **discount rate is lowered**, borrowing from the central bank becomes cheaper, encouraging banks to lend more and increasing the money supply.
- If the **discount rate is raised**, bank loans become costlier, slowing down the expansion of the money supply.

#### Reserve Requirements

- **Lowering the reserve requirement** allows banks to lend a larger proportion of their deposits, expanding the money supply.
- **Raising the reserve requirement** restricts bank lending, contracting the money supply.

Modern central banks rarely use reserve requirements as a regular instrument, relying more on OMOs and interest rates.

## Interest Rates: The Price of Money

One primary objective of monetary policy is to influence **interest rates**, most importantly the **short-term federal funds rate** (the rate at which commercial banks lend to each other overnight).

### How Changing Money Supply Affects Interest Rates

- **Increase in money supply** ⇒ More funds available to lend ⇒ **Decrease in interest rates**
- **Decrease in money supply** ⇒ Less funds available to lend ⇒ **Increase in interest rates**

### Transmission Mechanism

Interest rates influence broader economic activity through several channels:

- **Consumption**: Lower rates make borrowing cheaper (e.g., for car loans, mortgages), stimulating spending.
- **Investment**: Firms borrow more to expand when interest rates are low.
- **Exchange Rate**: Lower rates can weaken the currency, making exports more competitive.

## How Monetary Policy Affects the Economy

Let’s walk through the steps of **expansionary** and **contractionary** monetary policy.

### Expansionary Monetary Policy

Used to combat recession and high unemployment.

- Central bank **lowers interest rates** and/or **increases money supply**
- Encourages borrowing, spending, and investment
- Increases aggregate demand
- Leads to higher output, and (potentially) higher employment
- Risk: inflation could accelerate if output exceeds productive capacity

### Contractionary Monetary Policy

Used to fight inflation.

- Central bank **raises interest rates** and/or **reduces money supply**
- Discourages borrowing and spending
- Lowers aggregate demand
- Brings inflation under control but could slow growth and increase unemployment

### The AD-AS Model and Monetary Policy

Recall from Lecture 5: in the **Aggregate Demand - Aggregate Supply (AD-AS) model**:

- **Expansionary policy** shifts AD to the right → higher output and prices
- **Contractionary policy** shifts AD to the left → lower output and prices

Graphically:

$$
\text{AD}_0 \rightarrow \text{AD}_1 \quad (\text{with expansionary monetary policy})
$$

## The Limits and Challenges of Monetary Policy

- **Expectations**: If people expect inflation to rise, central bank actions may not be enough to keep demand in check (the "expectations channel").
- **Liquidity Trap**: When interest rates are near zero, further monetary easing may not stimulate extra borrowing.
- **Global Spillovers**: Policy in one country affects international exchange rates, capital flows, and other economies.

## Recent Examples

- **2008 Financial Crisis**: Central banks slashed interest rates and undertook "quantitative easing" (buying large quantities of assets) to inject money into the economy.
- **COVID-19 Pandemic**: In 2020, the Fed again cut rates to near-zero and purchased government securities at an unprecedented pace.

## Case Study: The Federal Reserve’s Open Market Operations

Suppose the Federal Reserve wants to lower short-term interest rates. It purchases $100 million in Treasury securities from commercial banks.

- The Fed credits the banks' reserve accounts with $100 million.
- Banks have more reserves and can lend more freely.
- Increased lending lowers the federal funds rate.
- Firms and consumers find it easier to borrow, stimulating spending and investment.

## Illustration: How the Money Multiplier Works

Whenever banks receive additional reserves, they can lend out a portion, creating new deposits.

Suppose the reserve requirement is 10 percent:

- Bank receives $1,000 deposit, must keep $100 in reserve, can lend $900.
- The $900 loan will be deposited elsewhere; of this $900, $90 must be reserved, $810 can be lent, and so on.
- The **money multiplier** is calculated as:

$$
\text{Money Multiplier} = \frac{1}{\text{Reserve Ratio}}
$$

In this case:

$$
\text{Money Multiplier} = \frac{1}{0.10} = 10
$$

So a $1,000 increase in reserves could, in theory, increase the money supply by up to $10,000.

## Basic Macroeconomic Models

### 1. The Keynesian Cross

The Keynesian Cross is a simple model to explain equilibrium output (GDP) in the short run via aggregate expenditure. It highlights how changes in spending can have multiplied effects on total income.

#### Key Features

- *Aggregate Expenditure (AE)* is the sum of consumption, investment, government spending, and net exports.
- Equilibrium GDP is at the intersection where AE = output (Y).

#### Graphical Representation

Imagine a graph with output (Y) on the x-axis and expenditure on the y-axis.
- The 45-degree line represents points where AE = Y.
- The AE curve’s intersection with the 45-degree line gives the equilibrium output.

#### Example

Suppose:
- $C = 200 + 0.6Y$ (consumption function)
- $I = 100$ (investment)
- $G = 150$ (government spending)
- $NX = 50$ (net exports)

Aggregate Expenditure:

$$
AE = C + I + G + NX = 200 + 0.6Y + 100 + 150 + 50 = 500 + 0.6Y
$$

At equilibrium: $Y = AE$

$$
Y = 500 + 0.6Y \implies 0.4Y = 500 \implies Y^* = 1250
$$

### 2. The IS-LM Model

The IS-LM model introduces the financial sector by linking goods and money markets.

- **IS Curve:** Equilibrium in the goods market (Investment-Saving)
- **LM Curve:** Equilibrium in the money market (Liquidity preference-Money supply)

#### Graphical Representation

- The IS curve is downward sloping (higher interest rates reduce investment, reducing output).
- The LM curve is upward sloping (higher income increases demand for money, raising interest rates).
- The intersection gives equilibrium interest rates and output.

#### Policy Analysis

- Fiscal expansion (increased G or reduced taxes): IS shifts right, higher output and interest rate.
- Monetary expansion (increased money supply): LM shifts right, lower interest rate, higher output.

### 3. Aggregate Demand–Aggregate Supply (AD–AS) Model Review

Recall from Lecture 5:
- The AD–AS model considers both price level and output.
- AD curve: Total demand at various price levels.
- AS curve: Total supply at various price levels (with short-run and long-run distinctions).

**Use Cases**
- Analyzing inflation-unemployment tradeoffs.
- Understanding responses to “shocks” (oil prices, policies).

### 4. The Solow Growth Model

Primarily a long-run model, the Solow model explains economic growth by:
- Capital accumulation
- Labor force growth
- Technological progress

**Key Equation:**

$$
Y = F(K, L)
$$

Where $Y$ is output, $K$ is capital, $L$ is labor.

The model predicts that, in the absence of technological progress, economies converge to a steady-state growth path.

## Evaluating Policy Effectiveness with Models

How do economists evaluate if policies “work”?

### Fiscal Policy

- **Short-run (Keynesian Cross, IS-LM):** Government spending increases can raise output and lower unemployment, especially when there is unused capacity.
- **Caveats:** Crowding out (higher G may lead to higher interest rates, discouraging I); lag times; political constraints.

### Monetary Policy

- **IS-LM and AD–AS Models:** Central bank actions (money supply, interest rates) directly move the LM curve or the AD curve.
- **Limitations:** Liquidity traps (when interest rates are near zero, monetary policy may be less effective).

### The Role of Expectations

- Modern extensions incorporate expectations; e.g., if people expect future inflation, policy can be less effective.

### Real-World Example

**The COVID-19 Recession (2020):**
- Governments enacted massive fiscal stimulus and unprecedented monetary policy easing (interest rates near or below zero, quantitative easing).
- Despite textbook predictions, inflation remained subdued for much of 2020-2021, challenging classic models.

## Recent Macroeconomic Trends

### 1. Interest Rates and Inflation

After years of low interest rates, many advanced economies faced a surge in inflation (2021-2023). Central banks responded by raising rates sharply.

#### Illustration

- US Federal Reserve: Nearly 0 percent rates in 2020 → over 5 percent by mid-2023
- Eurozone, UK, and other economies mirrored this trend.

### 2. Labor Markets

- “The Great Resignation” and labor shortages, even with rising wages in many sectors.
- Unemployment rates remained relatively low despite slowing growth.

### 3. Global Supply Chains

- Pandemic disruptions revealed vulnerabilities in global supply chains.
- Led to inflation in certain goods, and discussions of “reshoring” or diversifying suppliers.

### 4. Rising Public Debt

- Governments borrowed extensively to support households and businesses during 2020-2021.
- Raises questions about long-term debt sustainability.

## Globalization and Its Macroeconomic Effects

Globalization has major implications for national macroeconomic policies:

### Open Economy Macroeconomics

- **Expanded IS-LM (the Mundell-Fleming Model):** Considers capital flows and exchange rates.
- Outcomes depend on whether the exchange rate is fixed or floating.

### Policy Effectiveness in a Globalized World

- **Fixed Exchange Rate:** Limits effectiveness of monetary policy.
- **Floating Exchange Rate:** Limits effectiveness of fiscal policy due to exchange rate adjustments.

#### Example

Suppose a country uses fiscal expansion (increased G). In a floating exchange rate regime:
- Higher interest rates attract capital inflows.
- Currency appreciates.
- Net exports fall, offsetting the fiscal expansion.

### Global Shocks

- Oil price changes, financial crises, and pandemics now transmit rapidly among countries.
- Supply chain dependencies can amplify shocks.

### Trade-offs

- Globalization allows for efficiency and growth but exposes economies to external risks.
- Policy space can be "imported" or "exported" via global financial flows (e.g., contagion during financial crisis).
